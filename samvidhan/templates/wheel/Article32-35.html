Fundamental Rights: Articles 32-35

Article 32: Right to Constitutional Remedies

Article 32 of the Indian Constitution is a fundamental right that guarantees the right to constitutional remedies. It is considered one of the most crucial provisions in the Constitution as it empowers individuals to seek protection and enforcement of their fundamental rights directly from the Supreme Court of India. Article 32 grants every individual the right to move the Supreme Court for the enforcement of their fundamental rights. This means that if someone believes their fundamental rights have been violated, they can approach the Supreme Court directly for relief. It also ensures that not only do individuals have the right to move the Supreme Court, but the Court also has the power to issue appropriate orders, directions, or writs for the enforcement of fundamental rights.

Article 33: Restriction of Rights for Forces

Article 33 empowers the Parliament to make laws that would restrict the application of fundamental rights to a specific category of people including members of the Indian Army and intelligence organisations.Power of Parliament to modify the rights conferred by this Part in their application etc Parliament may, by law, determine to what extent any of the rights conferred by this Part shall, in their application to, the members of the Armed Forces or the members of the Forces charged with the maintenance of public order or persons employed in any bureau or other organisation established by the State for purposes of intelligence or counterintelligence or persons employed in, or in connection with, the telecommunication systems set up for the purposes of any Force, bureau or organisation referred to be restricted or abrogated so as to ensure the proper discharge of their duties and the maintenance of discipline among them.

Article 34: Restricting Rights During Martial Law

Article 34 of Indian Constitution allows the Parliament to impose restrictions on the rights conferred by Part III of the Constitution (Fundamental Rights) during the operation of martial law in any area. It provides indemnity (legal protection) to persons in respect of acts done in connection with the maintenance or restoration of order during martial law. The Parliament may by law validate any acts done under martial law that would otherwise be illegal.Article 34 provides a legal framework for imposing restrictions on fundamental rights during martial law, which is the imposition of direct military control in a situation of extreme emergency. It ensures that any actions taken to restore order and maintain peace during such periods, even if they contravene fundamental rights, are legally protected.

Article 35:Parliament's Power to Legislate on Rights

Article 35 of Indian Constitution safeguards the uniform enforcement of fundamental rights by placing specific matters under the legislative authority of Parliament. It relates to various provisions, including the restriction of fundamental rights for members of the armed forces (Article 33), members of forces maintaining public order, and the indemnity provided under Article 34 during martial law. The Article prevents state legislatures from enacting conflicting laws, thus maintaining consistency in the protection and enforcement of fundamental rights across all states and union territories of India.

Case Example : Sheikh Abdullah v. State of Jammu and Kashmir (1959).

Sheikh Abdullah challenged his detention under the Public Safety Act, 1978. The Supreme Court affirmed that the right to challenge detention is fundamental and must be available even in Jammu and Kashmir, reinforcing the applicability of Article 32.
